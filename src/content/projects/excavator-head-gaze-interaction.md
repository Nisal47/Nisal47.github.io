## Overview

During my internship with Trimble New Zealand's New Technology Innovation Group (Caterpillar Trimble Control Technologies, CTCT), I designed and developed a head-gaze interaction system for excavator operators, centred around a specialised circular radial UI. Interacting with Trimble's onboard tablet system required operators to step away from the excavator's primary controls, interrupting both workflow and situational awareness on active job sites. The project explored whether head-gaze tracking, paired with a purpose-built radial menu, could offer a more seamless, hands-free alternative.

## Process

The project began with understanding operators' existing workflows and the pain points around switching attention between machine controls and the tablet interface. From there, I designed interaction patterns suited to a head-gaze input model, developing a circular radial UI intended to make target selection fast and low-effort within the constraints of a moving cab environment. I worked iteratively with the team to align the gaze-tracking approach with the wearable device and display system, refining the interaction logic and UI through cycles of prototyping and testing.

Design decisions were shaped collaboratively with the team. Scrums and A/B testing sessions brought together perspectives from engineering, hardware, and UX, and much of the interaction and visual design evolved directly out of that group feedback. I am grateful for the team's input and support throughout, which shaped the final direction of the interface as much as any individual decision did.

## Outcome / Impact

The internship resulted in a working proof-of-concept demonstrating that head-gaze interaction, combined with a radial GUI, is a feasible hands-free input method for excavator cab environments. User studies with end users provided early validation of the interaction model's usability. The project contributed to the New Technology Innovation Group's ongoing exploration of novel interaction methods for Trimble's construction technology products, and was a strong introduction to designing for high-stakes, industrial UX contexts very different from typical consumer interfaces.
